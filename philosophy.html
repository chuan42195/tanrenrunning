The
Philosophy

Why small consistent gains beat big inconsistent efforts.
Every time.

01

On Compounding

There is a concept in finance called compound interest. Returns earned on an investment are reinvested, and those returns earn their own returns. The effect is invisible in the early stages and extraordinary over time. A 7% annual return on $10,000 becomes $76,000 over thirty years — not through dramatic events but through the quiet accumulation of consistent small gains.

Endurance performance works exactly the same way.

The athlete who trains at 70–80% of maximum heart rate for the majority of their sessions, week after week, month after month, is making a deposit into a physiological account that compounds invisibly. Mitochondrial density increases. Capillary networks expand. Cardiac stroke volume grows. Fat oxidation efficiency improves. None of these adaptations are visible in any single session. All of them show up on race day.

The athlete who trains too hard too often is not compounding. They are withdrawing. Every session above the aerobic threshold that is not specifically prescribed is a withdrawal from recovery, from adaptation, from the long-term account. It feels productive. It produces fatigue. It rarely produces the sustained upward trajectory that structured training does.

02

On Patience

The hardest thing I ask of every athlete I coach is to slow down.

Not permanently. Not on race day. In training.

Easy sessions should feel embarrassingly easy. The pace that feels productive for most recreational athletes is, in most cases, too fast to be genuinely aerobic and too slow to be genuinely speed work. It occupies a middle zone that is physiologically expensive and developmentally limited.

Slowing down in training is not a concession to fitness level. It is a strategic decision about where adaptation happens. It is the decision to build the base rather than perpetually test it.

Most athletes resist this for the first six to eight weeks. Almost all of them stop resisting it when they see what it produces.

03

On Consistency

The best training programme in the world, executed inconsistently, produces nothing.

A good training programme, executed with relentless consistency, produces extraordinary things.

This is not a motivational statement. It is a physiological one. Adaptation requires repeated stimulus over time. Miss a week and the stimulus resets partially. Miss two weeks and you are managing detraining. Miss a month and you are rebuilding.

Consistency is not about willpower. It is about building a programme that is sustainable enough to execute without interruption across months and years. That means appropriate load. Adequate recovery. Injury prevention baked into the structure from the beginning.

Consistency is the strategy. Not the discipline required to execute the strategy — the strategy itself.

04

On Multisport Athletes

Hyrox competitors and triathletes face a version of the layering challenge that is even more complex than the pure runner's. They are not building one system — they are building multiple simultaneously and trying to prevent each from undermining the others.

The principle does not change. The aerobic base is still the foundation. Intelligent load management is still the strategy. Consistency is still the differentiator.

What changes is the architecture — how the layers of each discipline are stacked relative to each other, and how the overall load is periodised across a race season that may include multiple events across multiple sports.

For most multisport athletes, the run is the discipline with the least structured development and therefore the greatest potential for improvement. Addressing it with the same intentionality applied to everything else is almost always where the biggest performance gain lives.

05

On the Long Road

Long distance and multisport events attract a certain type of person. Not the most naturally talented. Not the most physically gifted. The most willing to do work that produces no immediate result, trusting that the result is accumulating somewhere beneath the surface.

That willingness is the asset. The coaching is the architecture that makes it productive rather than just exhausting.

Tanren exists for that person. The one who is already doing the work and wants to make sure it is the right work. The one who understands intuitively that there are no shortcuts and is looking for someone who will confirm that and then show them exactly what the long road looks like.

The long road is the fast road.

Layer by layer.

Forged through consistency.

The Three Principles

Everything in a Tanren programme flows from these three foundations.

01

Aerobic Foundation First

Every programme begins with the base. Not the exciting work. The invisible work. The sessions that feel too easy but quietly raise your ceiling month after month.

02

Intelligent Load Management

More is not always better. The right amount at the right time separates improving athletes from injured ones. Load management is not caution — it is strategy.

03

Consistency Over Intensity

One brilliant session followed by two weeks of chaos produces nothing. Fifty ordinary sessions without missing one produces everything.
